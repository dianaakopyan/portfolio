Delivery management

The problem

Before touching any wireframes, we pulled three months of operational logs. The pattern was hard to miss.

Duplicate delivery documents appeared in nearly every week. Stock discrepancies clustered around the same moment almost every time — right after a supplier CSV import. Errors weren't random or caused by careless operators. They were systemic, and they traced back to three specific points in the delivery creation flow.

We didn't redesign delivery management from scratch. We found the three failure points and fixed them precisely.

Failure point 1 — building the document

The old process: enter items manually, calculate weights and dimensions separately, figure out costs afterward. Every step was disconnected from the next.

The new delivery form organizes items by storage spot and package type. Weight and dimensions per line. Unit price and totals calculated live as you add items. Receiving fee and monthly storage cost shown at the bottom — before you commit, not discovered afterward.

The by-package / by-item view toggle wasn't in the original scope. It came out of a workflow walkthrough with the operations team midway through the project. Half the room thought in pallets. The other half thought in individual items. Neither was wrong. We built both views on top of the same data model.

Failure point 2 — importing supplier data

Suppliers send CSV files. Those files have errors — wrong SKUs, formatting inconsistencies, items that don't exist in the nomenclature directory. The old system imported without any validation. Problems surfaced days later, after stock counts had already propagated incorrectly through the system.

The fix: validate every line before committing anything. Unmatched items appear in red with a tooltip that explains exactly why the system couldn't find them. The error list is downloadable — fix externally and re-upload, or add missing items manually.

That single change eliminated the discrepancy pattern we'd traced in three months of operational logs. Completely.

Failure point 3 — duplicate documents

Roughly 20% of delivery documents contained items that already existed in another open document for the same destination. It happened gradually — a manager would start a new document without checking whether one was already in progress. Each document looked correct in isolation, so no one flagged it.

A single modal intercepts before anything is committed. Create a new delivery document, or add to an existing one — existing documents shown with their number and date. Two options. One question. Duplicate documents stopped appearing within the first month.

Making conflict resolution explicit

A related edge case showed up in almost every usability session: someone would add an item that already existed in the document. About half of operators expected a quantity merge. The other half expected a new line. No dominant mental model — and designing for either assumption would have been wrong for the other half.

When a duplicate is detected, the system stops and presents two explicit options: keep the existing quantity, or add the item to a new storage location. The operator decides. No silent merges, no data loss.

Outcome

Duplicate delivery documents: eliminated within the first month. Stock discrepancies from CSV imports: went from a weekly occurrence to effectively zero.

The delivery creation flow became one of the highest-rated features in internal feedback — not because it was flashy, but because it stopped being a source of daily frustration.
